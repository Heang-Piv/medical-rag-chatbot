Title: Heart Disease
Source: Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC)
URL: https://www.cdc.gov/heart-disease/about/index.html
Fetched: 2026-07-16

Overview

Heart disease is a general term for several types of heart conditions, the most common being coronary artery disease, which affects blood flow to the heart and can lead to a heart attack. Heart disease is a leading cause of death for adults in the United States for most racial and ethnic groups.

Risk factors

Key risk factors for heart disease include high blood pressure, high cholesterol, and smoking. Other contributing conditions and behaviours include diabetes, overweight and obesity, unhealthy diet, physical inactivity, and excessive alcohol use. Many of these risk factors can be managed or improved through lifestyle changes and, when needed, medication.

Symptoms

Symptoms of a heart attack can include chest pain or discomfort, shortness of breath, pain or discomfort in the arms, back, neck, jaw, or stomach, and light-headedness, nausea, or cold sweats. Not everyone experiences the same symptoms, and some heart attacks are sudden, while others start slowly with mild pain or discomfort. Women may be more likely to experience atypical symptoms such as shortness of breath, nausea, or back or jaw pain.

Prevention

Making heart-healthy lifestyle choices can significantly lower the risk of heart disease. Recommended steps include maintaining a healthy blood pressure and cholesterol level, eating a diet low in saturated fat, trans fat, and sodium, getting regular physical activity, maintaining a healthy weight, not smoking, limiting alcohol use, and managing diabetes and stress. Regular checkups allow health care teams to identify and address risk factors before they lead to a cardiac event.

Statistics

Heart disease is one of the leading causes of death in the United States, and someone in the U.S. has a heart attack roughly every 40 seconds. Many cases of heart disease and heart attack are preventable through risk factor management, and CDC supports community and clinical programs that help people control blood pressure and cholesterol as key strategies for reducing the national heart disease burden.
