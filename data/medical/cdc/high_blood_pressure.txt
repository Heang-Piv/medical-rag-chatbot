Title: High Blood Pressure
Source: Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC)
URL: https://www.cdc.gov/high-blood-pressure/about/index.html
Fetched: 2026-07-16

Overview

High blood pressure, also called hypertension, is blood pressure that is consistently at or above 130/80 mm Hg. Blood pressure is the pressure that occurs when blood pushes against the walls of the arteries as the heart pumps. Normal blood pressure is less than 120/80 mm Hg. High blood pressure usually has no warning signs or symptoms, and many people do not know they have it; measuring blood pressure is the only way to know for sure.

Health care professionals generally use guidelines from the American College of Cardiology (ACC) and American Heart Association (AHA) to diagnose high blood pressure, using both the systolic (first) and diastolic (second) numbers.

Risk factors

High blood pressure usually develops over time and can occur because of unhealthy lifestyle choices such as not getting enough regular physical activity. Certain health conditions, such as diabetes and obesity, can also increase the risk of developing high blood pressure, and high blood pressure can occur during pregnancy.

Health consequences

The higher blood pressure levels are, the greater the risk for other health problems, including heart disease, heart attack, and stroke. High blood pressure can also cause heart valve disease and is linked to poorer cognitive function and dementia later in life, especially when it occurs in midlife. Adults with diabetes, high blood pressure, or both have a higher risk of developing chronic kidney disease than those without these conditions.

Facts and statistics

Nearly half of U.S. adults have high blood pressure (48.1%, about 119.9 million people), defined as a systolic blood pressure greater than 130 mm Hg or a diastolic blood pressure greater than 80 mm Hg, or taking medication for high blood pressure. In 2024, high blood pressure was a primary or contributing cause of over 680,000 deaths in the United States. Only about 1 in 4 adults with high blood pressure has their blood pressure under control. High blood pressure is more common in non-Hispanic Black adults (59.6%) than in Hispanic (44.9%), non-Hispanic White (44.8%), or non-Hispanic Asian (42.7%) adults, and more common in men (50%) than women (42.1%).

Prevention and management

Many people can lower their blood pressure into a healthy range, or keep it there, through lifestyle changes: getting regular physical activity (about 30 minutes a day, 5 days a week), eating healthy foods including plenty of fruits and vegetables while limiting sodium, maintaining a healthy weight, and not smoking. Self-measured blood pressure (SMBP) monitoring at home, combined with support from a health care team, helps more people successfully lower their blood pressure than clinic measurement alone. People diagnosed with high blood pressure should talk with their health care team regularly about their numbers and treatment plan, since many people need medicine in addition to lifestyle changes to reach a healthy blood pressure level.
