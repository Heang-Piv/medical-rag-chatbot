Overview

Asthma is a chronic disease that affects the airways in the lungs. The airways are tubes that carry air in and out of the lungs. With asthma, the airways can become inflamed and narrowed, and produce extra mucus, making it harder to breathe. Asthma can range from mild to severe, and while there is no cure, it can be managed effectively with the right care.

Symptoms

Common asthma symptoms include wheezing, coughing (especially at night or early morning), chest tightness, and shortness of breath. Symptoms can flare up during an "asthma attack," triggered by exposure to certain substances or conditions, and can vary in frequency and severity from person to person and over time.

Triggers

Common asthma triggers include tobacco smoke, dust mites, outdoor air pollution, pests such as cockroaches and mice, mold, pet dander, wood smoke, and strong cleaning or fragrance products. Respiratory infections, physical activity, cold air, and strong emotions can also trigger symptoms in some people. Identifying and reducing exposure to a person's individual triggers is a key part of asthma management.

Who is affected

Asthma affects people of all ages, but it often starts in childhood. Certain groups, including children, and some racial and ethnic minority populations, experience higher rates of asthma and more severe outcomes, often linked to disparities in environmental exposures and access to care.

Management and treatment

Asthma is managed through a combination of avoiding known triggers and using medications as prescribed, which typically fall into two categories: long-term control medications taken daily to reduce airway inflammation and prevent symptoms, and quick-relief (rescue) medications used to relieve symptoms during an asthma attack. An asthma action plan, developed with a health care provider, helps patients recognize worsening symptoms and know when to adjust medication or seek emergency care. With proper management, most people with asthma can lead active lives with few limitations.
