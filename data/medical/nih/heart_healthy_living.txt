Title: Heart-Healthy Living
Source: National Institutes of Health - National Heart, Lung, and Blood Institute (NHLBI)
URL: https://www.nhlbi.nih.gov/health/heart-healthy-living
Fetched: 2026-07-16

Overview

Heart-healthy living means adopting habits that help protect the heart and blood vessels and lower the risk of heart disease and stroke over a lifetime. Because many risk factors for heart disease, such as high blood pressure, high cholesterol, and high blood sugar, develop gradually and without noticeable symptoms, a heart-healthy lifestyle is important at every age, not only after a diagnosis.

Key components of a heart-healthy lifestyle

A heart-healthy eating pattern emphasizes fruits, vegetables, whole grains, lean protein, and healthy fats, while limiting saturated fat, added sugars, and sodium. Regular physical activity, such as at least 150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity aerobic activity, helps control weight, blood pressure, cholesterol, and blood sugar, and strengthens the heart muscle itself. Maintaining a healthy weight reduces strain on the heart and lowers the risk of related conditions such as high blood pressure and type 2 diabetes. Not smoking, and avoiding secondhand smoke, is one of the most effective steps a person can take to protect heart health, since smoking damages blood vessels and significantly raises the risk of heart attack and stroke. Managing stress, getting adequate quality sleep, and limiting alcohol intake also support cardiovascular health.

Monitoring and medical care

Regular health checkups that include blood pressure, cholesterol, and blood sugar screening allow risk factors to be identified and managed early, often through lifestyle changes alone, or with the addition of medication when needed. People with a family history of heart disease, or existing conditions such as diabetes or high blood pressure, may need more frequent monitoring and a personalized care plan developed with their healthcare provider.

Why it matters

Heart disease remains a leading cause of death, but research shows that a substantial share of heart disease and stroke risk is influenced by modifiable lifestyle factors. NHLBI supports research into heart-healthy behaviors, prevention strategies, and how these habits interact with genetic and social factors to affect long-term cardiovascular outcomes across different populations.
