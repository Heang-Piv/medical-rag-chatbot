Title: Depression

Overview

Depression (also called major depressive disorder or clinical depression) is a common but serious mood disorder that causes persistent feelings of sadness, emptiness, or hopelessness, and a loss of interest in activities once enjoyed. It is different from normal, temporary mood changes or brief emotional reactions to challenges in everyday life; to be diagnosed, symptoms must last most of the day, nearly every day, for at least two weeks and represent a change from a person's previous functioning.

Symptoms

Common symptoms of depression include persistent sad, anxious, or empty mood; feelings of hopelessness, worthlessness, or excessive guilt; loss of interest or pleasure in hobbies and activities; decreased energy or fatigue; difficulty concentrating, remembering, or making decisions; changes in sleep (difficulty sleeping or oversleeping); changes in appetite or weight; physical symptoms without a clear physical cause, such as headaches or digestive problems; and, in severe cases, thoughts of death or suicide. Not everyone with depression experiences every symptom, and severity, frequency, and duration vary by person and over time.

Causes and risk factors

Depression is thought to result from a combination of genetic, biological, environmental, and psychological factors. Risk factors include a personal or family history of depression, major life changes, trauma or chronic stress, certain medical conditions, some medications, and substance use. Depression can occur alongside other mental health conditions or serious physical illnesses, and can affect anyone regardless of age or background, though it is more common in some groups than others.

Treatment

Depression, even severe cases, is a highly treatable condition. The most common treatment approaches are psychotherapy (talk therapy, such as cognitive behavioral therapy), medication (such as antidepressants), or a combination of both; for some people, other approaches such as brain stimulation therapies may be considered when other treatments have not been effective. Most people with depression see improvement in symptoms with appropriate treatment, though it can take time to find the treatment approach that works best for a given individual.

If you or someone you know is struggling

Depression can affect a person's ability to function at work, school, and in relationships, and in severe cases can lead to thoughts of suicide. Anyone experiencing thoughts of suicide or self-harm should seek help immediately, such as by contacting a mental health professional or a crisis line. Early treatment leads to better outcomes, so recognizing symptoms and seeking help promptly is important.
