Title: Healthy Aging
Source: National Institutes of Health - National Institute on Aging (NIA)
URL: https://www.nia.nih.gov/health/healthy-aging
Fetched: 2026-07-16

Overview

Healthy aging involves adopting habits and routines that support physical, mental, and cognitive health as people grow older. While aging brings natural changes to the body and brain, research shows that many aspects of health in later life are influenced by modifiable behaviors, and it is never too late to adopt habits that support better health and independence.

Physical activity

Regular physical activity is one of the most important things older adults can do for their health. Exercise helps maintain muscle strength, balance, and flexibility, which can prevent falls, one of the leading causes of injury among older adults. Activity also supports heart health, bone density, weight management, and mental well-being. A well-rounded routine includes endurance (aerobic) activities, strength training, balance exercises, and flexibility work.

Nutrition

A healthy, balanced diet rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, lean protein, and healthy fats supports energy levels, immune function, and the prevention of chronic diseases such as heart disease, diabetes, and some cancers. Older adults may need to pay particular attention to adequate protein, calcium, vitamin D, and hydration, since needs and appetite can change with age.

Cognitive health

Keeping the brain active through mentally stimulating activities, staying socially connected, managing chronic conditions such as high blood pressure and diabetes, getting sufficient quality sleep, and avoiding smoking are all associated with better cognitive health in later life. NIA-supported research continues to study the relationship between lifestyle factors and the risk of cognitive decline and dementia, including Alzheimer's disease.

Preventive care

Routine health screenings, vaccinations, and managing chronic conditions with a health care provider are important parts of healthy aging. Older adults are encouraged to discuss an individualized preventive care plan with their doctor, since needs vary based on personal and family health history.

Social connection and mental health

Staying socially engaged with family, friends, and community activities supports emotional well-being and has been linked to better physical and cognitive health outcomes. Loneliness and social isolation, which become more common in older age, are associated with a range of negative health effects, making social connection an important focus of healthy aging efforts.
