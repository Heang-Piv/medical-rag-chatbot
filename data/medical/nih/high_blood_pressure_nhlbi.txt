Title: High Blood Pressure
Source: National Institutes of Health - National Heart, Lung, and Blood Institute (NHLBI)

Fetched: 2026-07-16

Overview

High blood pressure, or hypertension, occurs when the force of blood pushing against the walls of the arteries is consistently too high. Blood pressure is measured using two numbers: systolic pressure (the force when the heart beats) over diastolic pressure (the force when the heart rests between beats). Over time, uncontrolled high blood pressure can damage the heart and blood vessels and contribute to serious health problems such as heart attack, stroke, heart failure, and kidney disease.

Causes and risk factors

High blood pressure often has no single identifiable cause (called primary or essential hypertension) and tends to develop gradually over years, influenced by factors such as age, family history and genetics, being overweight or having obesity, an inactive lifestyle, a diet high in sodium, and heavy alcohol use. In some cases, high blood pressure is caused by an underlying condition such as kidney disease or hormonal disorders, or by certain medications (secondary hypertension).

Diagnosis

Because high blood pressure typically causes no symptoms, regular blood pressure checks are the only reliable way to detect it. A diagnosis is usually confirmed using multiple readings taken on different occasions, sometimes including at-home monitoring, to account for normal variation and to rule out "white coat" effects from measurement in a clinical setting.

Treatment and management

Treatment for high blood pressure typically combines heart-healthy lifestyle changes with medication when needed. Recommended lifestyle changes include following a heart-healthy eating pattern (such as the DASH diet, which emphasizes fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and low-fat dairy while limiting sodium), maintaining a healthy weight, getting regular physical activity, limiting alcohol intake, not smoking, and managing stress. When lifestyle changes are not enough to reach target blood pressure levels, healthcare providers may prescribe one or more classes of blood pressure medicines, which are often used in combination for better control.

Research

NHLBI supports research into the biological mechanisms of hypertension, new approaches to prevention and treatment, and the disparities in blood pressure control across different populations, aiming to improve long-term cardiovascular outcomes nationwide.
