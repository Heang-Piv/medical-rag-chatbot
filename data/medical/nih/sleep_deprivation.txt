Title: Sleep Deprivation and Deficiency
Source: National Institutes of Health - National Heart, Lung, and Blood Institute (NHLBI)
URL: https://www.nhlbi.nih.gov/health/sleep-deprivation
Fetched: 2026-07-16

Overview

Sleep deprivation occurs when a person does not get enough sleep, while sleep deficiency is a broader term that includes not getting enough sleep, sleeping at the wrong time of day, not getting good-quality sleep, or having a sleep disorder that prevents the body from getting the sleep it needs. Most adults need 7 or more hours of sleep per night for good health, though individual needs can vary, and needs are higher for children and teenagers.

Health effects

Ongoing sleep deficiency is linked to an increased risk of a range of chronic health problems, including heart disease, high blood pressure, kidney disease, diabetes, stroke, obesity, and depression. Sleep also plays an important role in brain function, including learning, memory, decision-making, and emotional regulation; insufficient sleep can impair concentration, reaction time, and mood. Sleep deficiency has been linked to a higher risk of injury in people of all ages, including a higher risk of motor vehicle and workplace accidents.

Causes

Sleep deficiency can result from a variety of factors, including lifestyle choices such as staying up late for work, school, or social activities; environmental factors like noise or light disrupting sleep; underlying sleep disorders such as insomnia or sleep apnea; and certain medical conditions or medications. Shift work, which requires people to work during the body's normal sleep hours, is a common cause of ongoing sleep deficiency.

Getting enough quality sleep

Good sleep habits, sometimes called sleep hygiene, can help improve sleep quality and duration. Recommended practices include keeping a consistent sleep and wake schedule, creating a comfortable and dark sleeping environment, limiting caffeine and alcohol close to bedtime, avoiding large meals and screens before bed, and getting regular physical activity during the day. People with persistent sleep problems, such as ongoing difficulty falling or staying asleep, loud snoring, or excessive daytime sleepiness, should talk with a health care provider, since these can be signs of a treatable sleep disorder such as sleep apnea or insomnia.

Research

NHLBI supports research into the biological role of sleep, the health consequences of sleep deficiency, and treatments for sleep disorders, aiming to better understand how sleep affects cardiovascular, metabolic, and overall health across the lifespan.
