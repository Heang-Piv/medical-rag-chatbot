Title: Influenza (seasonal)
Source: World Health Organization (WHO)

Fetched: 2026-07-16
Published: 28 February 2025

Key facts

There are around a billion cases of seasonal influenza annually, including 3-5 million cases of severe illness. It causes 290,000 to 650,000 respiratory deaths annually. Ninety-nine percent of deaths in children under 5 years of age with influenza-related lower respiratory tract infections are in developing countries. Symptoms begin 1-4 days after infection and usually last around a week.

Overview

Seasonal influenza (the flu) is an acute respiratory infection caused by influenza viruses, common in all parts of the world. Most people recover without treatment. Influenza spreads easily between people when they cough or sneeze; vaccination is the best way to prevent the disease. There are 4 types of influenza viruses (A, B, C, D). Influenza A and B viruses circulate and cause seasonal epidemics; only influenza type A viruses are known to have caused pandemics (e.g. the 2009 A(H1N1) pandemic).

Signs and symptoms

Symptoms usually begin around 2 days after infection and include sudden onset of fever, cough (usually dry), headache, muscle and joint pain, severe malaise, sore throat, and runny nose. The cough can be severe and last 2 weeks or more. Most people recover within a week without medical attention, but influenza can cause severe illness, pneumonia, sepsis, or death, especially in high-risk groups. In industrialized countries, most influenza deaths occur among people aged 65 years or older.

Who is at greater risk

People at greater risk of severe disease or complications include pregnant women, children under 5 years of age, older people, individuals with chronic medical conditions (cardiac, pulmonary, renal, metabolic, neurodevelopmental, liver, or hematologic disease), and individuals with immunosuppressive conditions or treatments (such as HIV, chemotherapy, or steroids). Health and care workers are at high risk of both acquiring and spreading infection.

Transmission

Seasonal influenza spreads easily, with rapid transmission in crowded areas including schools and nursing homes, via respiratory droplets from coughing or sneezing and via hands contaminated with the virus. In temperate climates, epidemics occur mainly during winter; in tropical regions, influenza may occur throughout the year with more irregular outbreaks. The incubation period is about 2 days, ranging from 1-4 days.

Treatment

Most people recover on their own. People with mild symptoms should stay home, rest, drink fluids, and treat symptoms like fever. People at high risk or with severe symptoms (pregnant women, children under 59 months, adults 65+, those with chronic illness, chemotherapy patients, or immunosuppressed individuals) should be treated with antiviral medications as soon as possible.

Prevention

Vaccination is the best way to prevent influenza. Safe and effective vaccines have been used for more than 60 years; because immunity fades over time, annual vaccination is recommended, especially for pregnant women, children aged 6 months to 5 years, people over 65, people with chronic medical conditions, and health workers. Other prevention measures include regular handwashing, covering coughs and sneezes, proper tissue disposal, staying home when unwell, avoiding close contact with sick people, and avoiding touching the eyes, nose, or mouth.

WHO response

WHO, through the Global Influenza Programme and the Global Influenza Surveillance and Response System (GISRS), continuously monitors influenza viruses globally, recommends seasonal vaccine compositions twice a year for the northern and southern hemispheres, guides tropical and subtropical countries on vaccine formulation, and supports Member States in developing prevention and control strategies.
